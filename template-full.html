More Stable Together: The International Dimensions of Authoritarian Rule
 

GIGA Focus | Global | Number 02 | June 2017 | ISSN 1862-3581
 

© Reuters / China Daily Information Corp. (CDIC)

﻿In 2017, approximately 60 per cent of the world’s population lives in countries categorised as “partly free” or “not free.” This is partially because of a decline in the quality of democracy, but primarily because authoritarian, dictatorial regimes persist. The domestic strategies of stabilisation that these regimes use are well-known, but authoritarian rule is also stabilised by four international dimensions.

  • Autocracy promotion is the direct, targeted support of authoritarian regimes by influential great or regional powers. The examples of China, Russia, and Saudi Arabia demonstrate that autocracy promotion primarily serves to stabilise loyal partners, rather than to develop a shared ideological project.
  • Authoritarian diffusion is the chronologically proximate emergence of similar non- or anti-democratic policies in different countries. An important example of such uncoordinated, not centrally steered diffusion processes is the global spread of state restrictions on non-governmental organisations and on internet activism.
  • Authoritarian learning happens when regimes observe, adapt, and implement the political strategies of others. Positive learning means following an external model, whereas negative learning denotes attempts to avoid others’ mistakes at home. During authoritarian regime crises, such as the Arab Uprisings of 2011, negative learning prevails.
  • Authoritarian cooperation between regimes takes place in diverse forms at the bilateral, multilateral and intergovernmental levels. Important sites of cooperation are regional organisations in which authoritarian regimes dominate, such as the Shanghai Cooperation Organization or the Gulf Cooperation Council.

Policy Implications:

These four international dimensions of authoritarian regimes need to be taken into account in dealings with dictators around the globe. Democracies are also actively involved in multiple ways, which is why it is vital that they critically analyse their own actions. Only when democracies are sensitised to how they (un)intentionally stabilise authoritarian regimes can they formulate foreign policies that are sustainably democratic-emancipatory and do not reproduce past mistakes.
